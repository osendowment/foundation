## OSE claims to “remain independent from political influence”, but how is this possible when decisions about which projects to fund can sometimes be inherently political, for example when deciding which projects are more valuable than other projects?

OSE focuses on a core set of principles:

* Other things being equal, software that is more widely used should receive more funding. Ideally, this will include
  both usage in the open source ecosystem, and usage in closed-source codebases.
* More funding should go to software that is at higher risk, whether because of maintainer burnout or other causes.

In this way, OSE does not make its own judgements about which software is valuable, but rather defers to the implicit
value judgements of the global software community.

## Why does cash membership require a donation of 1000 USD? Isn't this exclusionary?

The aim of this requirement is to ensure that decisions are made by individuals that have committed to caring about
OSE's goals as opposed to individual interests, and to ensure that discussions are focused by prioritising the voices of
passionate individuals. We recognise that not everyone can afford this, which is why in-kind membership is also
possible.

## Will there be any scaling problems due to Goodheart’s law (see [HN discussion][hn-discussion])?

It can be avoided in the future by additional dimensions for input metrics and large periods (years) for the analysis.
In the short term, there is no such problem at all. In the long term, people are capable of building robust systems with
automated inputs which can’t be gamed well — like Google search, YouTube and all large social networks, including
GitHub.

## How can OSE evolve in the long-term, especially in the AI-powered world?

We suppose that open source software will still be the cornerstone of the global tech infrastructure even 50–100 years
from now, as besides rapidly evolving tech components it is also a decentralised cultural and social phenomenon. It is
already everywhere and the entropy of the tech infrastructure will only increase. So there will be a need to sustainably
fund its maintenance. In the long term human maintainers will partially be substituted by AI agents, so the OSE targets
would be achieved even faster – some of the funds previously granted to people will instead go towards AI computing
costs.

## Proactive or reactive grantmaking?

The usual reactive collecting of grant applications (“only crying babies are fed”) looks OK, but not great. We will
likely proactively detect the most critical but underfunded OSS and offer grants to its maintainers.

## Endowments lock onto targets forever, but the tech world is dynamic. How will this be resolved?

* OSE does not attach itself to any targets with limited lifespans: software products, programming languages, people, etc.
* OSE is dedicated to the support of OSS, which is already used by 96% of software in the world and continues to grow exponentially.
* It's easier to imagine some notable universities vanishing in the next centuries than the world stopping its reliance on OSS.

## How will OSE work with existing OSS-focused non-profits?

* OSE is a new funding solution for the old OSS problem, using new resources
* Complementary to ecosystems (e.g. PSF and Ruby Central): supports their projects
* Non-competitive to generalistic players (e.g. LF and ASF): targets different donors

## How does OSE relate to cryptocurrency?

The Open Source Endowment, which is a regulated US nonprofit corporation, aims to make the open source ecosystem
sustainable using traditional funding methods. Our work is unrelated to cryptocurrencies. We aim to accept
cryptocurrency donations in the future, with proper KYC, but this is not a priority.

## What will OSE governance look like in the long-term?

* It is a self-governing community-led endowment foundation
* Everybody can become a member via the cash membership route by contributing at least $1000
* Members will gradually gain more power to elect board members
* If successful, the OSE leadership has nothing to do and almost entirely defers to the community

## How much money is needed to sustainably maintain the top 10K open source projects?

* Major OSS projects (e.g. Linux and Postgres) or commercially-backed (e.g. Pydantic) are well-funded
* OSE aims to systematically close the funding gaps which exist around this well-maintained core
* Even a small donation (a token of gratitude) can reduce the likelihood of maintainer burnout

## How should one refer to the Open Source Endowment?

* With a definite article when using the full name:<br>
  > “The Open Source Endowment launched today.”<br>
  > “I'm a member of the Open Source Endowment.”
* Without a definite article when using the abbreviation:<br>
  > “OSE launched today.”<br>
  > “I'm a member of OSE.”
